  Issue 1 — I-601A Provisional Waiver: US Citizen Spouse as Qualifying Relative; Role of US Citizen Children

  Statutory basis retrieved:

  INA §212(a)(9)(B)(v) (node 5395) reads in full:

  ▎ "The Attorney General has sole discretion to waive clause (i) in the case of an immigrant who is the spouse or son or daughter of a United States citizen or of an alien lawfully admitted for permanent residence, if it is established to the satisfaction of the Attorney General
  ▎  that the refusal of admission to such immigrant alien would result in extreme hardship to the citizen or lawfully resident spouse or parent of such alien. No court shall have jurisdiction to review a decision or action by the Attorney General regarding a waiver under this
  ▎ clause."

  Analysis — Does James (USC spouse) qualify?

  Unambiguously yes. The statute expressly names "the spouse... of a United States citizen" as a qualifying immigrant, and requires that extreme hardship fall on "the citizen or lawfully resident spouse or parent." James, as the US citizen spouse, is both the qualifying
  immigrant's relationship and the qualifying recipient of the hardship showing. This is the core of the I-601A petition.

  Analysis — Do the US citizen children satisfy or strengthen the standard?

  They cannot independently satisfy it. INA §212(a)(9)(B)(v) limits the qualifying relative for hardship purposes to a "citizen or lawfully resident spouse or parent." Mei's USC-born children are her children, not her parents. They are absent from the list of qualifying relatives
   under this provision. USCIS Policy Manual guidance (not retrievable from this database — verify at USCIS-PM Vol. 9, Pt. B or 8 CFR §212.7(e), neither of which is indexed) consistently confirms children are not qualifying relatives for the unlawful-presence waiver.

  However, the existence of the two USC children materially strengthens the extreme-hardship showing as to James. USCIS applies a "totality of circumstances" standard for extreme hardship, and hardship to non-qualifying relatives (including children) is relevant evidence insofar
  as it radiates through and compounds the qualifying relative's own hardship. The argument is that James, as the sole remaining parent in the US, would bear:

  - The full economic and logistical burden of single parenthood for two young children
  - Emotional and psychological distress from family separation and sole-parenting
  - Disruption of the established family unit and the children's wellbeing, which directly harms James

  These factors are well-established in extreme hardship adjudications. The children do not independently satisfy the standard; rather, their presence amplifies the hardship case for James.

  under final orders of removal (which affects Mei — see Issue 3).

  Issue 2 — Brief Departure and Re-entry Without Inspection Approximately 5 Years Ago: Clock Reset or Aggregation?

  This is the most legally dangerous issue in the fact pattern. The answer is a two-tier analysis.

  Statutory text retrieved:

  - INA §212(a)(9)(B)(i)(I) (node 5382): 3-year bar for >180 days but <1 year ULP + voluntary departure before commencement of proceedings
  - INA §212(a)(9)(B)(i)(II) (node 5383): "has been unlawfully present in the United States for one year or more, and who again seeks admission within 10 years of the date of such alien's departure or removal"
  - INA §212(a)(9)(B)(ii) (node 5384): "an alien is deemed to be unlawfully present in the United States if the alien is present in the United States after the expiration of the period of stay authorized by the Attorney General or is present in the United States without being
  admitted or paroled"
  - INA §212(a)(9)(C)(i)(I) (node 5398): "has been unlawfully present in the United States for an aggregate period of more than 1 year"
  - INA §212(a)(9)(C)(ii) (node 5400): exception requires "more than 10 years after the date of the alien's last departure" plus prior DHS consent before reembarkation

  Tier 1 — §212(a)(9)(B): The clock does not aggregate.

  Unlike §212(a)(9)(C)(i)(I), which explicitly uses "aggregate period," §212(a)(9)(B)(i)(II) does not — it measures ULP against a single departure or removal event. The 10-year bar under §(B) runs from "the date of such alien's departure or removal." Accordingly, each departure
  resets the §212(a)(9)(B) clock. If Mei departs for consular processing in 2026, the relevant unlawful presence period is measured from her last entry (approximately 2021 — the re-entry without inspection). Since she has been present without admission or parole since then
  (satisfying §212(a)(9)(B)(ii)), she has accrued approximately 5 years of unlawful presence — well over 1 year — meaning the 10-year bar under §212(a)(9)(B)(i)(II) would still apply upon her departure. The departure 5 years ago does not eliminate the §212(a)(9)(B) exposure; it
  merely resets its measurement baseline.

  Tier 2 — §212(a)(9)(C): The departure 5 years ago may have triggered a permanent bar.

  This is the critical exposure. At the time of Mei's departure approximately 5 years ago, she had been accruing unlawful presence continuously since her initial entry on the B-2 visa. Assuming she overstayed within months of entry 8 years ago, she had approximately 3 or more
  years of accumulated unlawful presence at the point of departure — far exceeding the "aggregate period of more than 1 year" threshold in §212(a)(9)(C)(i)(I). She then re-entered the United States without inspection.

  Section 212(a)(9)(C)(i)(I) bars any alien who "has been unlawfully present in the United States for an aggregate period of more than 1 year... and who enters or attempts to reenter the United States without being admitted." Mei's unauthorized re-entry after accumulating more
  than 1 year of aggregate unlawful presence facially satisfies both prongs of §212(a)(9)(C)(i)(I).

  The only statutory escape from §212(a)(9)(C) is the §212(a)(9)(C)(ii) exception (retrieved text, node 5400):

  ▎ "Clause (i) shall not apply to an alien seeking admission more than 10 years after the date of the alien's last departure from the United States if, prior to the alien's reembarkation at a place outside the United States..., the Secretary of Homeland Security has consented to
  ▎  the alien's reapplying for admission."

  If Mei departs for consular processing in 2026, her "last departure" would be 2026. She cannot satisfy the "more than 10 years after last departure" requirement until approximately 2036. The §212(a)(9)(C) permanent bar has no conventional waiver (the §212(a)(9)(C)(iii) VAWA
  waiver does not apply here).

  Strategic implication for the attorney: The brief departure 5 years ago, if established, may convert Mei's situation from a surmountable 10-year-bar scenario (waivable via I-601A with extreme hardship showing) into a 10-year mandatory wait outside the US followed by a DHS
  consent application with no guaranteed approval — no visa, no I-601A, no return until at earliest 2036. Counsel should carefully assess the evidentiary strength of this claimed departure before raising it in any filing or administrative record. If the departure cannot be
  proven, Mei faces only the §212(a)(9)(B) 10-year bar with the I-601A pathway available.

  Issue 3 — In Absentia Removal Order: I-601A Bar; Motion to Reopen for Lack of Notice

  Database limitation notice: INA §240 (8 USC §1229a), including §240(b)(5)(C)'s in absentia rescission provisions, is not indexed in this database. The analysis below is based on retrieved provisions from INA §212 and the general statutory framework, but counsel must verify the
  motion to reopen procedure and standards directly from INA §240(b)(5)(C) and 8 CFR §§ 1003.23 and 1240.8(d).

  Does the in absentia removal order categorically bar the I-601A?

  Yes, under the current regulatory framework — but not necessarily permanently. 8 CFR §212.7(e)(4)(i) (not indexed; verify from primary source) generally disqualifies applicants who are subject to a final order of removal, deportation, or exclusion from filing Form I-601A. The
  rationale is that the I-601A is designed for aliens who have a pathway to consular processing and need the waiver approved before departure; aliens under final removal orders are in a different procedural posture. An in absentia removal order, once the 30-day appeal period to
  the BIA has passed without appeal, is a "final order" for this purpose.

  This bar is the procedural gateway problem: until the removal order is resolved or vacated, Mei cannot access the I-601A at all, regardless of the strength of her extreme hardship showing.

  Motion to Reopen — INA §240(b)(5)(C) (not retrieved from database):

  There are two recognized statutory bases for rescinding an in absentia order (verify from primary source, INA §240(b)(5)(C)):

  1. Exceptional circumstances (§240(b)(5)(C)(i)): Must be filed within 180 days of the order. This avenue is time-barred — Mei's order was entered 2 years ago.
  2. Failure to receive notice (§240(b)(5)(C)(ii)): No time limit. This is Mei's viable pathway if the facts support it.

  The lack-of-notice basis requires showing that the alien "did not receive notice" as required under INA §239(a)(1) (also not indexed; verify from primary source). Relevant considerations:

  - Service to last address of record: INA §239(a) requires that the NTA be served in person or, if impracticable, by mail to the alien or attorney at the most recent address provided. If the NTA was mailed to an address Mei had previously reported but since vacated — without Mei
   having updated her address with DHS — the analysis turns on whether DHS properly served the NTA at the address of record. Courts have generally held that service at the address of record is procedurally sufficient even if the alien no longer resides there, unless the alien had
   provided an updated address that DHS ignored.
  - Key factual questions: (a) Was the address on the NTA Mei's then-current address as known to DHS? (b) Had Mei ever filed any paperwork with USCIS or ICE updating her address? (c) Did the hearing notice (if separate from the NTA) also go to the old address?
  - Practical note: The Pereira v. Sessions (2018) and Niz-Chavez v. Garland (2021) Supreme Court decisions established that an NTA without a specified hearing date/time is deficient for stop-time rule purposes, but courts have been divided on whether a deficient NTA
  automatically defeats a valid in absentia order. The BIA in Matter of Yauri, 25 I&N Dec. 103 (BIA 2009) addressed the standard for lack-of-notice rescission motions.

  If the motion to reopen is granted: Mei would be back in active removal proceedings. This creates its own I-601A complication — 8 CFR §212.7(e)(4)(i) (verify from primary source) may also bar I-601A applicants who are in pending removal proceedings. Counsel should explore
  whether proceedings can be administratively closed to allow the immigrant visa/I-601A pathway to proceed, or whether the immigration judge would grant a continuance pending consular processing.

  If the motion to reopen is denied: The final order stands, the I-601A is unavailable, and the only consular processing route involves filing I-212 (permission to reapply) for the §212(a)(9)(A) bar and I-601 (at consulate, not provisional) for the §212(a)(9)(B) bar — a
  significantly more difficult posture.

  Issue 4 — INA §212(a)(9)(A): Does the Permanent Bar Apply If Mei Never Physically Departed?

  Statutory text retrieved:

  - INA §212(a)(9)(A)(ii)(I) (node 5377): "has been ordered removed under section 1229a of this title or any other provision of law"
  - INA §212(a)(9)(A)(ii)(II) (node 5378): "departed the United States while an order of removal was outstanding"
  - INA §212(a)(9)(A)(iii) (node 5379): "Clauses (i) and (ii) are waived for an alien who seeks admission within the restricted period if, before the alien's re-embarkation abroad or before attempting entry from a foreign contiguous territory, the Attorney General has given
  consent for the alien to reapply for admission"

  Note: The bar period for §212(a)(9)(A)(ii) (10 years from departure for "other aliens," 20 years for second removal, permanent for aggravated felony) is contained in the preambular text of node 5376, which is not a leaf node and was not fully retrieved. Counsel must verify the
  exact bar period from the statutory text directly.

  Does §212(a)(9)(A) currently bar Mei while she remains in the US?

  No. §212(a)(9)(A) is a ground of inadmissibility — it operates at the moment the alien "again seeks admission" upon re-entry. An alien who is inside the United States and has not sought re-entry is not currently being "admitted" and is therefore not subject to the §212(a)(9)(A)
   bar in the present tense. The removal order affects Mei's removability and her status, but the §212(a)(9)(A) bar is prospective and becomes operative only when she departs and applies for a visa or seeks entry at a port of admission.

  What happens upon departure for consular processing?

  Once Mei departs, §212(a)(9)(A)(ii)(I) applies because she "has been ordered removed... under any other provision of law" — the in absentia order satisfies this condition regardless of the fact that she was never physically expelled. The in absentia order is a formal legal
  order of removal; it does not require physical deportation to trigger §212(a)(9)(A)'s bar. §212(a)(9)(A)(ii)(II) would also apply independently if she "departs while an order of removal is outstanding," which she would be doing in the consular processing scenario.

  The §212(a)(9)(A) bar period for "other aliens" (non-arriving alien context, as Mei entered on a B-2 visa) begins running from the date of departure. Mei would face the §212(a)(9)(A) bar when she applies for her immigrant visa at the US consulate in China.

  How §212(a)(9)(A) and §212(a)(9)(B) interact:

  They are cumulative, not mutually exclusive. Mei would potentially face both bars simultaneously upon departure:

  Each bar requires its own separate remedy. The §212(a)(9)(A) bar has no waiver in the conventional sense; instead, relief is obtained via Form I-212 (Application for Permission to Reapply for Admission), which is discretionary and does not have the "extreme hardship" standard —
   it is evaluated under a broader totality-of-circumstances test. Note that §212(a)(9)(A)(iii) requires that consent be obtained before re-embarkation, so the I-212 must be filed and approved before Mei departs if proceeding via the provisional pathway, or filed at the consulate
   if proceeding via consular I-601.

  Critically, §212(a)(9)(A) is not the "permanent bar" colloquially referenced in this area of law — it has a defined term (10/20 years) and is waivable via I-212. The term "permanent bar" in immigration practice refers specifically to §212(a)(9)(C), which has no time limit and
  no available waiver for Mei's circumstances. The interaction is therefore:

  - If §212(a)(9)(C) does not apply (i.e., the 5-year-ago departure cannot be established): Mei faces §212(a)(9)(A) and §212(a)(9)(B) bars, both surmountable with appropriate applications and a strong hardship record.
  - If §212(a)(9)(C) applies (departure established): All three bars apply simultaneously, with §212(a)(9)(C) imposing the most restrictive floor — Mei cannot be admitted for at least 10 years after her departure regardless of how strong the §212(a)(9)(A) and §212(a)(9)(B)
  showings are.
